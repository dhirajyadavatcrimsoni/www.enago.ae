How do a PhD in Physics and IT and an MBA go together? Is it a synergistic relationship or are the two mutually exclusive?
  Physics is responsible for the development of the hardware that makes IT possible, so understanding physics and its fundamental limitations leads to a better understanding of the limits of IT. An MBA (in Technology Management) allows you to cross the divide between fundamental science and applied technology that is commercially viable. Thus, for those interested in commercializing technology, an MBA in Technology Management combined with a knowledge of physics allows you to understand the technology landscape, both from a technical viewpoint as well as from a business viewpoint.

Are there any special challenges in working with someone whose first language isn't English?
  I have found that language and culture has a strong influence on how people form their thoughts, so knowledge of foreign languages (e.g., Latin languages, Asian languages...) can be helpful in deciphering what an author is trying to say. For example, a French author will insert many parenthetical statements before delivering the main point, whereas a native English speaker will tend to deliver the main point first, following which the details will be given. This understanding can help an editor reformulate the author's message in English without losing what is written "between the lines."
   
What are your focus areas when it comes to editing papers of non-native English speakers? Are there any common mistakes and tendencies in academic papers?
  My main focus is clarity. I strive to ensure that the edited paper clearly conveys the technical matter so that no confusion or misinterpretations arise. Following this, I edit the paper so that it is easy to read, which means that the reader should be able to read the paper without having to re-read paragraphs or sentences because of confusing or misleading writing.
   
How do you deal with papers which have a lot of technical matter?
  I find it helpful to divide such a paper into logical and digestible sections and subsections. Combined with a table of contents or a good introduction, this is an efficient way to convey technical matter.
   
How would you rate the importance of having a good graduate degree for editing subject-specific documents?
  In highly technical fields such as the physical sciences, it is very important for the editor to have a good grasp of the overall field to navigate the minefield of potential misunderstandings. These fields (physics, materials science, engineering...) have developed a very rich language of their own in which a layman can easily be lost.
   
How do you think of importance of having editor who is specialized in the same field as the paper?
  While ideal, this is not vital, nor is it always possible. However, for a paper in the hard sciences, it is important that it be edited by someone who has extensive experience in the hard sciences. For instance, a condensed-matter physicist can do an excellent job of editing a paper that deals with geology, astrophysics, or optics. However, a physicist would not be the best choice to edit a paper in biology or chemistry.
   
Any advice for Japanese researchers writing their first academic papers?
  I would advise the Japanese author to strive for clarity and to avoid the temptation to introduce complicated phrases in the hope of impressing the reader.

What commonalities have you observed in research papers which are in high-impact-factor journals?
  These papers tend to focus on a single, well-defined research question of high importance and deliver some key information that leads to progress on this question. They tend not to be expansions on topics for which the fundamental or key understanding is already acquired.
