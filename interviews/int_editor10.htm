A double major in English and Anthropology, this editor has applied her talents to editing documents from a diverse array of subjects, ranging from sociology to biosciences to clinical medicine. A member of the American Medical Writers Association (AMWA), she has worked in a senior capacity for several well-known publishing firms.
 
Please describe the 9 years of editing experience you have had in your own words.
  After university, I started working as a writer for a medical publishing company and during that time I wrote and edited numerous articles relating to medicine and medical research and development. I then began working as a freelance editor editing manuscripts for non-native English authors and I have been doing this work for the past 8 years. Recently, I have returned to university to complete a further postgraduate degree specialising in molecular microbiology.
   
Please enumerate the subjects opted by you when undertaking your post-graduate (Master’s) degree. Which of these do you consider your fundamental subjects and have primary expertise in?
  I took papers in Coastal Marine Ecology and Aquaculture, Microbial Interactions and Ecosystems, Applied Microbiology and Biotechnology and Natural Resource Management. I also completed a review paper on Wine Microbial Ecology.
   
Have you edited papers of native and non-native English speakers? Are there any special challenges in working with someone whose first language isn't English?
  Yes, I have edited both types of papers. I find that when I edit manuscripts from non-native speakers I need to pay particular attention to the use of articles (the, a etc.), prepositions (e.g. in, on) and subject-verb agreement. Cultural differences also become apparent because in English scientific writing we tend to be very formal whereas sometimes non-native authors who wish to express their gratitude to colleagues tend to use emotive words and sentences such as "We wish to express our very great gratitude towards ...". In English, we would usually simply say "We wish to thank `x' for their assistance".
   
How do you deal with papers which have a lot of technical matter?
  These types of papers generally need an extra "read-through". I also often note down the important points on a piece of paper and then use that to try and follow the logic of the manuscript to ensure that the argument is both clear to a reader and also valid scientifically.
   
Are there any resourceful sites that you visit frequently while editing a manuscript?
  Google is amazing! I also often use university databases such as Scopus and Biological Abstracts if I need to do some extra reading around a topic.
   
How would you define substantive editing, and differentiate it from copyediting?
  I think that with substantive editing I pay more attention to the "science" of the paper and I don't just focus on the English as I do with a copyedit. I will make an extra effort to ensure that the manuscript presents a logical argument and that the methods used are relevant and generally accepted. Of course no editor can be an expert in every single topic so this type of editing is not as thorough as a peer review would be but it is still useful for picking up "scientific" errors as opposed to English errors alone.
   
Given a chance to work on a pet editing/publishing project of your choice, what would you choose and why?
  I enjoy working on manuscripts relating to medical conditions (especially cancer and immunology) and food/plant microbiology as these fields are the ones that I have the most background knowledge in due to my previous work history and my current postgraduate studies.
   
How did you begin your stint with Medical and Technical Writing? Which subjects did you specialize in?
  I was really simply lucky enough to "fall" into the job straight out of university. I have always received good grades in both English and Biology so I was very happy to be able to combine the two fields. Academically, I have focused on Ecology and Microbiology but in my work I have tended to focus more on medicine.
   
According to you, what are the major differences between Medical Editing and Medical Writing? And does having experience in one paradigm help the other?
  In many ways the differences between medical editing and medical writing are the same as those between a substantive edit and a copyedit. To edit a manuscript properly it is very important that you have the background knowledge necessary to not only make sense of the paper in front of you but also to see where it fits in relation to other papers in the field. This knowledge helps you to determine whether the results reported and discussed in the paper are valid given the methods used. A medical writer, on the other hand, does not need the same level of experience or breadth and depth of background knowledge. However, the two are mutually beneficial as a medical writer can easily become a medical editor as he/she increases their knowledge of their field of interest and the basic writing skills gained as a writer are still used when one becomes an editor.
   
Can you tell us about your process of writing a paper? For example, 1. Pick a topic, 2. Research, 3. Write a draft by putting all the information to a paper, 4. Selection of information 4. Rough draft, 5. Edit, 6. Submit.
  This is mostly intuitive. I find I tend to work from "chaos" into "order". Generally some topic will peak my interest and I will then read up on that topic, sometimes extensively and sometimes not if I lose interest. Once I have identified a topic I am interested in and have gained a reasonable amount of knowledge about through my reading, I will plan out a suitable study, ensure that the appropriate resources and funding are available, work out a timeline, proceed with the study, analyse the results and then write the paper. The first step when writing the paper is to gather all the relevant materials (notes, research papers, data etc.). I then try and "categorize" all of these and allocate them to appropriate sections of the paper e.g. "Introduction", "Results", "Discussion" etc. This can be achieved simply by writing a keyword relating to that piece of information on a piece of card and then putting each of the cards into a specific pile that represents the category. Once I have all of the information in the appropriate category, I then try and arrange it into a logical order. I then write the first draft of the paper - generally by taking the "keywords" or "bullet points" from the cards and making these into full sentences complete with references etc. The most important things to know are that each paragraph should only discuss one topic (which should be introduced in the first sentence) and that the paper as a whole is actually an argument trying to show what your study has contributed to the field and why it is important. I generally revise the first draft myself then have someone else look at the paper before I complete the final draft and submit it.
   
You also mentioned that you have been a Laboratory Demonstrator/Tutor in a reputed university. What kind of knowledge does that give?
  Demonstrating at a university is actually quite a challenging experience because you can never predict just what questions students are going to ask you or what actions they are going to perform in a laboratory situation. The questions they ask are often ones you have never asked yourself and so you actually learn more about the reasoning behind the methodologies you use in certain contexts and also more about their limitations!
   
In medical field, can you explain about the importance of having editor who is specialized in the same field as the paper?
  As I mentioned earlier, I think it is important for a medical editor to focus on one or two fields simply because of the increased breadth and depth of knowledge this type of specialisation can achieve. Our knowledge of the etiology and mechanisms underlying numerous medical conditions is growing daily and this growth is often due to rapid improvements in technology. It is important for an editor to have an understanding of the terminology surrounding these new technologies as well as of the techologies themselves in order for him/her to be able to correctly analyze whether the methodology used in a study is valid for achieving its stated aims. Without sufficient background knowledge of the "norms" of the field, an editor will not be able to complete a thorough edit of a manuscript.
   
What commonalities have you observed in research papers which are in high-impact-factor journals?
  I think that these types of papers generally either 1) ask new questions or 2) find new ways of answering existing questions.
   
Finally, any advice for Japanese researchers writing their first academic papers? Are there any common mistakes and tendencies in academic papers by Japanese researchers in particular that you observed?
  I think the most important thing is to make sure that the "science" behind the paper is valid. It is also important to target the right journal so reading a number of journals is important to see not only what types of papers those journals publish but also the style of writing they use. I think the Japanese researchers make similar mistakes to most non-native English speakers e.g. the incorrect use of articles and prepositions in particular. I have also noted that sometimes they mix up `l' and `r' e.g. they will write `proriferate' instead of `proliferate'. I would like to point out though that generally their standard of English is good - it may not be "native"-level English but it is usually good enough for me to understand what they are trying to say and I am sure that my Japanese would be far more incomprehensible!
