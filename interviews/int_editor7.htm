A double major in English and Anthropology, this editor has applied her talents to editing documents from a diverse array of subjects, ranging from sociology to biosciences to clinical medicine. A member of the American Medical Writers Association (AMWA), she has worked in a senior capacity for several well-known publishing firms.
 
You have specialized in Pharmacology and have over 10 years of editing experience. Please describe this journey in your words.
  I carried out my doctorate in pharmacology at University College London, where I also completed post-doctoral research. This was followed by Fellowship at the Scripps Institution at the University of California. I then returned to the UK to follow a career in academic publishing. This led to me becoming a freelance medical writer and medical editor.
   
How has the field of Pharmacology evolved in the past few years? What does the future looks like?
  Pharmacology has become closely tied to molecular biology. This trend is bound to continue with advances in the latter.
   
How do you keep yourself abreast of the latest happenings in your field? Do you attend seminars or conferences?
  I attend seminars and conferences throughout the UK and Europe.
   
What draws your personal attention the most now in subjects of medicine area? This can be your ongoing research or recent discovery in this field.
  With respect to internal medicine, respiratory medicine and oncology are the most interesting areas with respect to research.
   
We see that you are a member of eminent editing and writing associations. How has this affiliation helped you in the long course of editing?
  Being a member of such organizations enables me to obtain their newsletters. Such communications enable me to keep abreast of latest developments with respect to: software changes that may help writers and editors; changes in academic publishing; and the economic aspects of being a freelancer.
   
Are there any resourceful sites that you visit frequently to corroborate your edits?
  Google Scholar and PubMed
   
You have also prepared and published reports for various editorial boards. What are your general observations on the various papers you might have come across, especially those written by non-native English-speaking authors?
  I am continually impressed by the quality of medical/scientific contributions prepared by authors whose first language is not English. I get very irritated by people who say that 'his/her English is terrible'. How many English people could write a very technical article in another language and another script?
   
Are there any special challenges in working with someone whose first language isn't English?
  Working with such content for long periods of time makes one aware of what the author is trying to say. As long as one looks closely at each sentence then there is no problem.
   
Can you tell us about the importance of having the editor who is specialized in the same field as the paper?
  The editor will have a much better background knowledge of the subject. This will aid analyses of the article (particularly with verifying the 'Discussion' section of articles). He/she will also instantly pick up incorrect terms and jargon.
   
How do you deal with papers which have a lot of technical matter?
  I deal with such content evry day because I am a medical writer.
   
We see that you have worked for eminent publishing houses. How has this affiliation helped you in the long course of editing?
  I have a very good knowledge of the mechanics of academic publishing, particularly peer review. This has helped me enormously with respect to advising clients on the chances of acceptance of their contribution. It is also why I am an Editorial Consultant for various cleints.
   
What commonalities have you observed in research papers which are in high-impact-factor journals?
  (i) Very good English; (ii) Very thorough methodology of the study; (iii) Detailed discussion of results; (iv) Detailed 'Discussion' section; (v) Highly referenced.
   
Finally, any advice for Japanese researchers writing their first academic papers?
  Ask someone who has published in an English-written journal to look at all versions of article and make suggestions/edit the article. This person should look very carefully at the article before passing it on to Enago!
