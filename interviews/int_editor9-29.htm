A double major in English and Anthropology, this editor has applied her talents to editing documents from a diverse array of subjects, ranging from sociology to biosciences to clinical medicine. A member of the American Medical Writers Association (AMWA), she has worked in a senior capacity for several well-known publishing firms.
 
You have a very interesting profile here with more than 25 years of editing experience. Please describe this journey in your own words.
  I got into writing in my first job after graduating in zoology, producing summaries of research in veterinary medicine and pest control for a major pharmaceutical company. After that I spent many years with a team of information workers helping researchers in animal breeding and genetics keep abreast of world literature in this field. Meanwhile I moonlighted as a copy-editor and proofreader for medical journals, but eventually gave up full-time employment to concentrate on freelance copy-editing and proofreading, which allowed me to live and work anywhere I chose. I chose to move from the UK to live in Australia, but having found the pull of family ties too strong, I am now back in the UK.
   
How has the field of Zoology evolved in the past few years? What does the future looks like?
  When I was a student, the subject still involved a good deal of natural history and studies of all the different animal groups and their comparative anatomy. Many hours were spent each week in the museum examining and drawing preserved specimens. The proportion of zoologists' time devoted to general topics such as physiology and genetics, behaviour and ecology has increased enormously since those days. I think the future of zoology will see increasing attention to matters that are of pressing concern to humanity, such as biodiversity, the biology and evolution of disease organisms, and the chemical and mathematical aspects of evolution, taking advantage of the advances that are being made in other natural and applied sciences.
   
How do you keep yourself abreast of the latest happenings in Zoology?
  I use the internet to look at sites related to the subject, read magazines such as New Scientist and Scientific American, follow relevant radio and TV programmes, and try to take in as much new knowledge as I can from the editing assignments I receive from Enago.
   
What do you think is the “next big thing” in Zoology?
  I hope there will be some kind of coalescence between molecular genetics, mathematics and animal behaviour to produce a broader theory of evolution.
   
Given a chance to work on any R&D project/lab in the world, which one would you choose and why?
  A project to apply the principles of mathematical and statistical genetics to the study of language evolution.
   
Are you a fan of Wikipedia, especially the Biology portal there (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology_portal)? Are there any other resourceful sites that you visit frequently?
  I find the basic Wikipedia site extremely useful.
   
Are there any special challenges in working with someone whose first language isn't English?
  Yes! You have to try to think outside the box of English sentence structure and be aware that a word a writer has got from a dictionary might be quite wrong.
   
Can you tell us about the importance of having editor who is specialized in the same field as the paper?
  Apart from the need to know the correct terminology and to have a good idea of what is already known in the subject, when editing you have to fill in the many gaps that arise between the author's ideas and their ability to express them in a foreign language. Only by having some knowledge of the subject can you hope to complete the picture correctly.
   
What are your focus areas when it comes to editing papers of non-native English speakers? Are there any common mistakes and tendencies in academic papers?
  Depending on their native language, authors frequently do not make clear whether they are talking about one or several items, which can be quite a critical aspect. It is also important not to read the text superficially, because many plausible-sounding phrases turn out to be completely wrong or to give the opposite sense to the correct one. Many authors are unfamiliar with the conventions of paper-writing in English language journals.
   
How do you deal with papers which have a lot of technical matter?
  Slowly and methodically.
   
We see that you are an Advanced Member of SfEP, a highly recommended society for academic editors. How has this affiliation helped you in the long course of editing?
  Through an email system, members of the society give freely of their expertise not only in the intricacies of the English language and editing, but also in a wide variety of specialised subjects. I often take advantage this expertise and even share my own on occasion, and have benefited greatly from the courses the society runs to improve the professional standards of editors and proofreaders. Working towards Advanced Membership has been a valuable incentive to improve my professional standards. Freelancing can be a lonely life, but the SfEP helps to alleviate this through its system of local groups, which hold regular meetings, and through its annual conference, where members meet to socialise as well as to share their expertise and learn from expersts in various fields.
   
What commonalities have you observed in research papers which are in high-impact-factor journals?
  Authors have clearly taken great care to hone their writing to comply with the highest standards of scientific communication (for instance, they use the simplest language consistent with clear expression of their ideas) and the journal's requirements, and to eliminate all but the essentials. They are aware of what their fellow scientists are likely to know and what they are not likely to know, and don't waste words.
   
Finally, any advice for Japanese researchers writing their first academic papers?
  Read some high-impact journals and note how economical the authors are with their writing. Decide what are your important ideas and what are just padding. Clarify your thoughts, arrange them in the most logical sequence, and express them in sentences with a simple structure. Learn how to express singular and plural nouns and verbs in English.
