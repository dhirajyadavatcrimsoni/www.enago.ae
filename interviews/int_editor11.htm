A double major in English and Anthropology, this editor has applied her talents to editing documents from a diverse array of subjects, ranging from sociology to biosciences to clinical medicine. A member of the American Medical Writers Association (AMWA), she has worked in a senior capacity for several well-known publishing firms.
 
You have a very interesting profile here with personal experience in Veterinary Medicine and Surgery. How has this helped with your tryst with editing and proofreading documents of these subject areas?
  By background in veterinary science has helped immensely with my second career in publishing. Most genres of STM are familiar territory for me, as in the UK veterinary and medical students share a common grounding in zoology, botany, chemistry and physics. Without this background, I would find it very difficult to maintain a career in STM publishing.
   
How has the field of Medicine and Surgery evolved in the past few years? What does the future looks like?
  In recent years the field of medicine and surgery has increasingly focused on the area of stem cell technology toward the treatment of many conditions that up to now have proved difficult or impossible to treat, and I believe this is our most promising tool in the future to tackle the conditions that still bedevil so many of the world's population. I am very optimistic that, within a generation, this technology will have revolutionised medical science.
   
How do you keep yourself abreast of the latest happenings in your field? Do you attend seminars or undergo continued medical education, or what is commonly known as CME?
  I regularly read veterinary and medical journals, and attend courses periodically.
   
What draws your personal attention the most now in subject areas of medicine?
  As stated above, I follow very closely the advances continuously being made in stem cell research; I am also interested in paramedical disciplines, such as physiotherapy, holistic healing and occupational therapy.
   
Your employment history states that you were responsible for managing your veterinary practice. How has that experience helped you in day-to-day editing?
  Very much so. When self-employed, there are many aspects involved in running a successful organisation: self-discipline; making optimal use of time available; ensuring constant communication with both clients and colleagues; ability to 'think on one's feet'; taking decisions sometimes when nobody else is there for advice or help; keeping a healthy balance between work and leisure.
   
Are there any resourceful sites that you visit frequently to corroborate your edits?
  I presume you mean websites? I regularly 'Google' scientific terms to verify their accuracy, including those websites listed in the Enago handbook for editors. The 'Pubmed' website is invaluable for checking the accuracy of, or finding missing details of, journal articles and authors' names. I also regularly use the website <www.bookfinder.com>, which is extremely useful when finding information on published textbooks.
   
Are there any special challenges in working with someone whose first language isn't English?
  Obviously, the better the writer's standard of English, the easier the edit, and having edited and proofread manuscripts by writers from many parts of the world, one begins to pick up on the various inconsistencies in phrasing and use of language that require correction. When the standard of English is really poor, editing can be a very slow business, with some sentences requiring to be read perhaps 3 or 4 times before the meaning becomes clear. Sometimes one has to admit defeat, and insert a query for the author as to what is meant.
   
Can you tell us about the importance of having the editor who is specialized in the same field as the paper?
  When an editor is not familiar with a particular field, editing can be very difficult, although use of the Internet certainly helps considerably in understanding the text. Conversely, when the editor is familiar with the subject matter, errors are less likely to be left unchanged, with a final version that should be, if not perfect, very good.
   
What are your focus areas when it comes to editing papers of non-native English speakers? Are there any common mistakes and tendencies in academic papers?
  Generally, the scientific terminology is fairly accurate, and it is the shorter words – articles, pronouns, etc. – that are incorrect. With regard to Japanese authors, because of the differences between their language and English, the chief focus areas are articles and verb tenses. After a little practice, one's eye automatically looks for those mistakes. Sometimes it proves easier to rewrite a sentence or small passage to convert the text into good English.
   
How do you deal with papers which have a lot of technical matter?
  A large amount of technical matter is not a problem for me, unless the subject matter is one not familiar to me, e.g. advanced physics, astronomy. As stated above, there are many web-based resources where I can go for assistance if needed.
   
We see that you have worked for eminent publishing houses. How has this affiliation helped you in the long course of editing?
  As in most professions, the more experience you have, the more efficient you become at the tasks required. Each publishing house has its own 'house style', and frequently particular jobs require some departure from that style, so I have had to adapt to using several different editing styles, sometimes over the course of a single day! Everyone I have worked with has been very knowledgeable and helpful, and when I have made mistakes, they have showed me where I have gone wrong and pointed me in the right direction. All the publishers I have worked for require high standards, so I endeavour to meet those standards with every job I undertake. Also, I am constantly learning, and working for different houses has given me a broad base of experience.
   
What commonalities have you observed in research papers which are in high-impact-factor journals?
  The vast majority of the research papers I have edited are rigorously scientific in their approach, i.e. the texts are not 'padded out' with irrelevant or misleading facts. No doubt many authors have vast experience of having their work published, and this makes the task of editing that much easier. Very occasionally an article is poorly researched and/or written, and that can be far more difficult to edit; fortunately, this is rare.
   
Finally, any advice for Japanese researchers writing their first academic papers?
  1. If possible, have the paper read and corrected by someone (preferably with some knowledge of the subject matter) with a good understanding of the English language. 2. Check with the intended journal what criteria are required: maximum number of words in the article? maximum number of words in the Abstract? what should the headings be? 3. Set out the article with a logical structure; read other articles in your field to ascertain the 'flow' and structure that are recognised as the norm.
