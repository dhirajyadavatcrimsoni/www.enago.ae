A double major in English and Anthropology, this editor has applied her talents to editing documents from a diverse array of subjects, ranging from sociology to biosciences to clinical medicine. A member of the American Medical Writers Association (AMWA), she has worked in a senior capacity for several well-known publishing firms.
 
Now-a-days, many theoretical concepts of economics are observed and understood by the common man. According to you, which among them is the most common and influential concept?
  Although I've worked extensively in business and government and have taught college economics, I have a somewhat eccentric answer for this question. What most concerns me is that the "common man"--and, for that matter, people with more profound professional training--is using economics to reinforce the conclusion he wants to be true. In other words, the concept that influences them is the concept they want to believe, whether it's Keynesian, monetarist, or supply-side doctrine. Economics should not be a discipline employed to confirm your prejudices.
   
“Recession” has made its presence felt worldwide, leaving no industry unaffected. Have you come across any instances recently where recession has affected the editing industry?
  I think the most notable effect has been in corporate writing and editing staffs, whose numbers have dwindled as the result of recession-inspired cutbacks.
   
How do you keep yourself abreast of the latest happenings in Economics?
  I read a great deal from the contemporary press--Wall Street Journal, Financial Times--and have the television financial channels--Bloomberg, CNBC--on all the time, except when I work. I subscribe to several fee-based economics and investment websites. Working for Edisense is an education in itself, as I deal with manuscripts concerning many subjects every month.
   
What do you think is the “next big thing” in Economics?
  Economics is an explosive field, and it's almost impossible to predict what the next great trends will be. Personally, I would like to see economic scholars turn more attention to the link between ethics and judgment in decision-making. We have had many discussions of decision-making under conditions of uncertainty, but--particularly given what the world has just gone through--I'm seeing no research that investigates how financial institutions could possibly have made some of the decisions they have made. As for specific subjects, I think we will see more scholars turn to the issue of consumption taxes. I would like to see more discussions concerning the economics of poverty. Micro-lending is fascinating to me, and I hope to see more written about it.
   
Are you a fan of wikipedia, especially the Economics portal there (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics_portal)? Are there any other sites that you visit frequently?
  I absolutely love Wikipedia, and I've bookmarked a number of other on-line business and financial sites. I consult them constantly.
   
Do you think having a wide range of subject expertise works in the editor’s advantage? If yes, how has that helped you in this field?
  Economic and financial topics are becoming less general and increasingly specialized. I am fortunate to have a fairly extensive experience, courtesy of my years spent in the Senate and Treasury Department, with some rather intricate subjects, including taxes, trade, and investments. I find that the common denominator increasingly is mathematics, and, again, I'm fortunate to have most of the common economics math in my background.
   
Could you give us an example of an assignment that challenged your skills as an editor?
  Almost every Edisense assignment is a substantial challenge in some way, because authors' arguments often are intricate, and they are trying to break new ground with their manuscript. Without doubt, the longer assignments are the most difficult. In addition to presenting the standard issues of forcing each sentence to pull its weight, long manuscripts inherently have more complicated structural problems. In particular, authors tend to repeat their points, which is certain death for getting your manuscript accepted by a harried, over-worked publisher. Longer manuscripts have a tendency to become circuitous, yet they, in particular, must be the most linear arguments.
   
Looking into your educational background in Journalism, could you suggest any particular skill that proves extremely useful in the field of Editing?
  I follow two rules with every manuscript. First is what I call the First Rule of 1: try to take one word out of every sentence, one sentence out of every paragraph, one paragraph out of every section. Then repeat until you can't delete anything more. Second is what I call the Second Rule of 1: In so far as possible, make sure there's only one main idea and its relevant modification in a sentence.
   
Can you explain the importance of having editor who is specialized in the same field as the paper
  There are two issues here. Obviously, a scholar wants his or her manuscript edited by someone at least modestly conversant with the subject. However, what authors often most need is an editor whose basic skill is simply recognizing if they have made a logical, understandable, persuasive argument. And that is not always a matter of having intricate technical understanding of a subject.
   
Can you tell us about your process of writing a paper? For example, 1. Pick a topic, 2. Research, 3. Write a draft by putting all the information to a paper, 4. Selection of information 4. Rough draft, 5. Edit, 6. Submit
  Although I've published nine full-length books and hundreds of articles, I do very little original writing now that I'm semi-retired. I have the luxury now of writing only about what I am truly interested in learning. As every Edisense client knows, writing is hard work. Writing (and editing, too) is not a "knack," like spinning a beach ball on your nose. I no longer have to write for money and professional recognition, and that gives me a special connection with those who do.
   
What are your focus areas when it comes to editing papers of non-native English speakers? Are there any common mistakes and tendencies in academic papers?
  I'd give non-native speakers the same advice I'd give native speakers: rely on strong verbs, and pick the simple word over the fancy one. A strong verb will triumph over any other collection of words you can put on the page, and, in general, authors use elaborate words to disguise an ill-formed point.
   
If any, what have you observed in research papers which are in high impact factor journals have in common?
  Without doubt, the major journals want, above all, a clear, unambiguous statement of what an author's exact findings are. They also want to know how an author's work adds to the literature of a discipline. In sum, the great articles have two things in common: clarity and contribution.
