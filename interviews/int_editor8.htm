A double major in English and Anthropology, this editor has applied her talents to editing documents from a diverse array of subjects, ranging from sociology to biosciences to clinical medicine. A member of the American Medical Writers Association (AMWA), she has worked in a senior capacity for several well-known publishing firms.
 
Could you give us an example of an assignment that challenged your skills as an editor?
  I edited a book on consciousness and meditation last summer, and the initial draft was quite disorganized. The author could not put her thoughts down clearly on the page. Although the material was quite good, the writing was disjointed. I treated it as a "puzzle," and moved around or deleted sections within each chapter until it made sense. The author was amazed. She'd done the actual writing, but she was shocked by how much sharper the manuscript was with some restructuring. Ever since I first took Latin in high school and realized that language could have the quality of a puzzle, I've been drawn to working with language in various ways.
   
What is your view on the current trends in children's literature from Enid Blyton to J.K.Rowling?
  I'm afraid I mainly know about the Harry Potter series from the critics I read when the books first came out. Although the books were quite popular, my understanding is that they were not especially literary. Other than that, I don't read children's literature. I sometimes read reviews of children's books.
   
What was your PhD dissertation about? What led you to choose this subject?
  My PhD dissertation was about French and German women authors who wrote short stories and novels about the anti-Nazi resistance in World War II. I lived in West Berlin in 1973, and became interested in the German language and in literature after that experience. I can't really say why, but I was fascinated by the whole period between World War I and World War II. There was a lot of cultural ferment in Europe at that time, and many modernist trends were emerging. I always thought I would have liked to live in Europe then. It was a time that really tested people, too, in many ways, and I often wondered if I could have met some of those challenges.
   
Culturalism is manifesting itself in diverse arts all over the world. How do you keep track of latest trends in genres and styles of writing?
  I read cultural and literary reviews to keep up with new authors in many parts of the world. In the last few years, I've become interested in authors whose backgrounds extend across cultures, or whose fiction unites themes of East and West, like Salman Rushdie, Michael Ondaatje, Jhumpa Lahiri, and Orhan Pamuk.
   
Google's book digitization has come under fire from various academic circles. What is your perspective on the issue?
  I'm opposed to this in general. Although Google's stated motivation is to open up the world of books to "everyone everywhere," there is always money--not altruism--at the root of actions like this. So I think Google will be the primary beneficiary, not "readers." I might be somewhat more sympathetic to their aims if I hadn't found my own book on Google Books. It appeared there without my knowledge. Neither Google nor the publisher asked my permission to digitize the book. Also, as someone who has taught college students, it's difficult to get across that these aren't actually books (pages are missing) that can be cited in research papers. I personally like books I can hold in my hands.
   
Which literary age fascinates you and why?
  I'm still fascinated by the period between the two wars. I recently read Suite Francaise, a novel by a Russian Jew whose family emigrated to France to escape the Russian Revolution. The author had sketched out a plan to write a quartet of novels about the interwar years in France and the Occupation. She only finished two books before she was deported to Auschwitz, where she died. This book was found 64 years later. I also love many contemporary authors who treat multicultural themes. I'm so intrigued by the globalization of our world today.
   
What advice would you give an author who is attempting a comparative study of the arts?
  As someone who completed a PhD in comparative literature, I'd advise that the most important thing is to have a deep and thorough knowledge of the cultures one is studying. A grounding in the language, cultural background, history, and politics of a country is essential to a comparative study.
   
Within the field of editing, do you think that having a wide range of subject expertise works to the editor’s advantage? If yes, how has it helped you in this field?
  I do think it is advantageous to have a wide range of subject expertise. I read pretty widely, so even if I am assigned to edit an article on a topic I have not studied as an academic, I can usually establish some kind of "foothold" based on my own reading.
   
Can you explain the importance of having an editor who is specialized in the same field as the paper?
  I believe that an editor who is a specialist in the same field as the author of the paper can look more closely at the writing in terms of the global issues and also the finer details. If someone has that kind of expertise, it's almost second-nature to think about an article in terms of not only how the argument is put together but also how the language is constructed.
   
What are your focus areas when it comes to editing papers of non-native English speakers?
  I generally approach editing as if I am teaching, so rather than simply revising the errors, I try as much as possible to explain to the writer the reasoning behind the choices I've made. At least, I attempt to think in that way as I work. I usually focus first on the general meaning, to get a sense of the author and the gist of the paper, and then go back to work on the smaller, sentence-level details.
   
What commonalities have you observed in research papers which are in high-impact-factor journals?
  The prose is clear, and the authors have developed a sense of their audience. They understand how to set up and support an argument. They also are aware of how important it is to polish the language as much as possible.
   
Finally, any advice for Japanese researchers writing their first academic papers? Are there any common mistakes and tendencies in academic papers by Japanese researchers in particular that you observed?
  My best advice--and something I've done in my own work--is to look at some articles in the field to get a good understanding of how the research is put together, and how an effective argument is constructed. This isn't to say anyone should "copy" another author's work, but rather that these models can give an author a good sense of how to organize and present one's ideas. Aside from common grammatical errors, what I've noticed most often is that some authors seem unaware of the expectations for scholarly writing and unaware of the needs of their potential readers. They should always try to imagine themselves in their readers' place and try to write from that perspective.
